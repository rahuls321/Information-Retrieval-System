Vantablack is a brand name for a class of super-black coatings with total hemispherical reflectances (THR) below 1.5% in the visible spectrum. The original Vantablack coating was grown from a chemical vapour deposition process (CVD) developed by Surrey NanoSystems in the United Kingdom and is one of the darkest coatings known, absorbing up to 99.965% of visible light (at 663 nm if the light is perpendicular to the material).[4][5] The coatings are unique in that they are not only super-black but that they retain uniform light absorption from almost all viewing angles. Original CVD Vantablack is no longer manufactured for commercial applications as it has been superseded by Vantablack spray coatings that offer similar optical performance in key parts of the electromagnetic spectrum.[6]
The name is a combination of the acronym VANTA (vertically aligned nanotube arrays)[7] and the color black.

Properties[edit]
  Vantablack grown on metal foil
CVD Vantablack is composed of a forest of vertical tubes "grown" on a substrate using a modified chemical vapor deposition process. When light strikes Vantablack, instead of bouncing off, it becomes trapped and is continually deflected amongst the tubes, eventually becoming absorbed and dissipating into heat.[8]
CVD Vantablack was an improvement over similar substances developed at the time. Vantablack absorbs up to 99.965% of visible light and can be created at 400 °C (752 °F). NASA had previously developed a similar substance that was grown at 750 °C (1,380 °F), so it required materials to be more heat resistant than Vantablack.[8]  Darker materials are possible: in 2019, MIT engineers developed a CVD material which reflects a tenth of the amount of light that Vantablack reflects.[9]
The outgassing and particle fallout levels of Vantablack are low compared to similar substances, which makes it more commercially viable. Vantablack also has greater resistance to mechanical vibration, and has greater thermal stability.[10]

Development[edit]
Early development was carried out at the National Physical Laboratory in the UK;[11] the term "Vanta" was coined some time later.[12] Vertically aligned nanotube arrays are sold by several firms, including NanoLab,[13] Santa Barbara Infrared[14] and others.[15]
The Vantablack name is trademarked by Surrey NanoSystems Limited,[16] and has been referenced in three patents registered in the United States Patent and Trademark Office.[17]

Applications[edit]
As one of the darkest materials, Vantablack has many potential applications, such as preventing stray light from entering telescopes, and improving the performance of infrared cameras both on Earth and in space.[15] 
In addition to directly growing aligned carbon nanotubes, Vantablack is made into two sprayable paints with randomly-oriented nanotubes, Vantablack S-VIS and Vantablack S-IR with better infrared absorption than the former. These paints require a special license, a temperature of 100–280 °C, and vacuum post-processing.[18] Surrey NanoSystems also markets a line of non-nanotube sprayable paints known as Vantablack VBx that are even easier to apply.[19]

Artistic and decorative use[edit]
  BMW X6 Vantablack at Frankfurt Motor Show 2019
Vantablack S-VIS, a sprayable paint that uses randomly-aligned carbon nanotubes and very high levels of absorption from ultraviolet to the terahertz spectrum, has been exclusively licensed to Anish Kapoor's studio for artistic use.[20]
Nanolab, a Waltham, Massachusetts-based carbon nanotube manufacturer, partnered with Boston artist Jason Chase to release a nanotube-based black paint called Singularity Black.[21] During the first showing of the colour, Chase, alluding to Vantablack, stated that "its possibilities have been stunted by not being available to experiment with", and Singularity Black's release was important to create access.[22][23]
The manufacturer claims that Vantablack is subject to export controls by the UK, and due to its physical requirements and thermal characteristics, the original Vantablack is not practical for use in many types of art.[24]
Vantablack VBx2, a variant of the non-nanotube Vantablack VBx that is optimized for large area spraying, was used in a "Vantablack pavilion" at the 2018 Winter Olympics.[25]
BMW unveiled an X6 concept with Vantablack paint at the Frankfurt Auto Show in September 2019; however, the company does not plan on producing the color on production models of the X6.[26]
French musician Gesaffelstein used Vantablack VBx2 as part of his stage decoration during his Coachella 2019 performance. [27][28]

Commercial production[edit]
The first orders were delivered in July 2014.[15] In 2015, production was scaled up to satisfy the needs of buyers in the aerospace and defense sectors.[citation needed]
